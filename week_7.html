Week 7 – Project Pivot and Redefining Direction

Tags: project pivot, beneficiaries, Blender, city design, education

This week marked a significant shift in my project direction. After attending several peer presentations and speaking in detail with my supervisor, I realised that the original target audience—skilled professionals in related industries—would require a far more technically advanced and precise simulation. Additionally, the product I was proposing would need to be something entirely new to them, which posed challenges in terms of feasibility and scope.

Following these discussions, I redefined my project's purpose and shifted the focus to a more appropriate beneficiary group: young children and teenagers. The aim became educational—helping this demographic to better understand natural disasters through interactive, visual learning tools.

Initially, I explored the idea of creating a city in a 3D simulation environment and layering natural disaster scenarios over it. This led to transitioning from Maya to Blender as my primary tool, due to Blender’s flexibility and available extensions. I experimented with add-ons such as Blosm, Blender GIS, and a few online city generators. However, I quickly discovered limitations: many of these tools provided pre-built cityscapes, which made it difficult to integrate realistic and responsive disaster simulations.

As a result, I decided that I would need to build my own city from scratch—which brought me back to a concept I had previously explored: Omashu, a fictional city with strong architectural and geographic identity.

To support this change, I conducted additional literature review focused on seismic-resistant buildings. Why buildings? Because after analysing real-world data and creating visualisations, I noticed that the greatest financial impact of natural disasters is often related to the destruction and rebuilding of buildings. Therefore, I chose to focus on structures first—developing earthquake-aware architecture—and gradually expand the simulation to include the surrounding environment.

Two examples that inspired my design direction were Meteora in Greece (known for its cliff-perched planning) and Dukezong in China (noted for its traditional timber-based seismic architecture). These references provided cultural, geographical, and structural insights that will influence how I design my fictional city moving forward.

Furthermore, I decided that the project would culminate in a website that serves as an educational guide for young people. The platform will offer a variety of engaging learning methods, including interactive elements, videos of simulations, and real-world data overlays to provide a realistic understanding of natural disasters. The goal is to create an accessible, informative, and visually compelling experience that communicates both the risks and recommended actions during emergencies.
